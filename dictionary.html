Mutual Fund

Investing

Definition

A professionally managed investment vehicle that pools money from multiple investors to purchase securities like stocks, bonds, money market instruments, and other assets. This allows individual investors to gain exposure to a diversified portfolio of securities that might be difficult to create independently.

How It Works

When you invest in a mutual fund, you buy units of the fund at the current Net Asset Value (NAV). Your money, along with that of other investors, is used by professional fund managers to create a portfolio based on the fund's stated objectives. The returns from the fund's investments are distributed to investors in proportion to their unit holdings.

Examples

  • SBI Bluechip Fund - A large-cap equity fund that invests primarily in well-established companies.
  • HDFC Balanced Advantage Fund - A hybrid fund that invests in both equity and debt instruments.
  • Aditya Birla Sun Life Liquid Fund - A liquid fund that invests in very short-term money market instruments.

Types of Mutual Funds

Equity Funds

Invest primarily in stocks, with the goal of capital appreciation. Higher risk but potential for higher returns.

Debt Funds

Invest in fixed-income securities like government bonds, corporate bonds, and other debt instruments. Lower risk with more stable returns.

Hybrid Funds

Invest in a mix of equity and debt instruments, balancing risk and returns.

Index Funds

Passively managed funds that mirror a specific market index like the Nifty 50.

Key Points to Remember

  • Mutual funds are managed by professional fund managers.
  • They charge an expense ratio, which is a percentage of your investment deducted annually.
  • Direct plans have lower expense ratios than regular plans as they don't involve distributor commissions.
  • Returns are not guaranteed and depend on market performance.
  • Different mutual funds have different risk profiles and investment objectives.
